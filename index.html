Autonomous Machine Unlearning

Securing financial privacy in convex optimization models via Projected Gradient Ascent.

By Justin Minseob Seo  |  Mentor: Jun-Kun Wang

The "Right to be Forgotten"

Regulations like GDPR require organizations to delete user data upon request. However, exact model retraining is computationally prohibitive, scaling at O(N).

Current heuristic unlearning methods inject stochastic noise into the model. This damages overall utility and critically fails to offer verifiable mathematical guarantees of privacy compliance.

Surgical Unlearning via PGA

We isolate the fundamental dynamics of unlearning by applying Projected Gradient Ascent (PGA) to strictly convex environments. The objective is to maximize error exclusively on a targeted "forget batch" while preserving decision boundaries for retained data.

w_{t+1} = w_t + η ∇L_forget(w_t)

Geometric Verification

Is unlearning a precise mathematical projection, or just random noise?

Comparing the weight update to the target's feature vector yields a Cosine Similarity of 1.0. PGA perfectly mirrors the geometric subspace of the forgotten data, confirming it is a strict mathematical projection.

The Stopping Point Paradox

Ascending the gradient indefinitely causes catastrophic forgetting. Real-world systems lack a "Gold Standard" retrained model to flag the optimal stopping point.

We discovered that monitoring the Validation Loss on the Retain Set acts as a highly reliable, deployable blind metric to halt unlearning autonomously.

Privacy Auditing

To verify compliance, we subjected the unlearned model to a Membership Inference Attack (MIA).

At the deployable stopping point (Step 31), the model's confidence on the targeted users plummeted from 99% (memorization) to ≈ 50% (random guessing), successfully neutralizing the privacy risk.
